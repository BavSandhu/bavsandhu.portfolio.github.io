Ideation Time!

To help us ideate for our possible design solutions, we used the technique SCAMPER. We chose and shortlisted over 40 ideas into some of the following:

  • Provide quizzes to review concepts
  • Inform professors of how students are doing
  • Connect with peers

This helped us finalize our idea: a mobile app for students. As all our users told us they would have their phones nearby during online lectures, we thought we could use it as a way students can use their phones as an asset rather than a distraction.

1) HOMEPAGE

Where students can access the feature they're looking for (before, during, or after class and study buddy).

2) BEFORE Lectures

To help students prepare for their lectures beforehand. It includes quick lecture summary videos and a brief list of the main concepts to be learnt in class.

3) DURING Lectures

Students can anonymously let the professor know about their comfortability with class content; they can rate how they feel with a sad, medium, or happy face, on a specific slide's content in real time

4) AFTER Lectures

Students can rate their comfortability with concepts (similar to the during class page) by giving a final anonymous rating about the day's content to professors. They can also review the concepts they learned from the lecture with a quiz.

5) HELP

Based on how students rate topics, they are matched with a study buddy and can chat with one another.

Identifying Areas to Further Improve

Using the wizard of oz experiment, we tested the before, during, after and study buddy interfaces of the app to see if each page is digestible, understandable, and able to do what the users want it to do. The following is what we changed:

  • BEFORE Lectures
    • Organize the lecture summaries by the course and date
  • AFTER Lectures
    • A page where students can choose which course's concepts they want to review
    • Instead of having students rate concepts in order, have them rate each concept using stars (1-5 stars)
  • DURING Lectures
    • Include the slide people are rating on the app
    • Once a student rates how they feel about concepts on a slide, display a notification saying 'Rating Submitted'
  • STUDY BUDDY
    • Change the 'Help' section to the name 'Study Buddy'
    • Remind students that their study buddy changes after a week
    • Allow users to make an avatar to make more comfortable

Testing with UX Guerrilla & Think-Aloud

We used the think-aloud protocol and UX guerrilla testing technique to help us better understand their interactions with our design solution. It ensured we obtained valuable insights through their experiences with our interface design so that we could further create iterations. We found:

1) ‚ÄúIs there a way I can view future lecture summaries or past ones?‚Äù

Users felt it would be helpful to prepare for future lectures as well instead of just upcoming ones. They also wanted the ability to view past lecture summaries for reference.

2) ‚ÄúI want to send anonymous questions to the professor‚Äù.

Users want the option to ask questions to professors anonymously. This way if they provide a low rating of their comfortability with a concept, they can ask for clarification in a way that doesn't bring attention to them.

3)‚ÄùWhy was this person chosen to be my study buddy?‚Äù

Users express they want transparency into why their study buddy was chosen for them.

4)"Will I still be able to chat with past study buddies?

Users want a way to keep connected with past study buddies so they don't lose friendships, and in turn resources.

My Angle

> The COVID-19 pandemic caused many academic institutions to suddenly shift to a new learning environment mid-semester. As online learning is heavily operated through technology and on technical devices such as a student's computer, it can cause communication overload. This essentially means that it can make it difficult for users to remain concentrated and can negatively impact work efficiency, accuracy and overall performance. An article on student's perceptions about online learning states, ‚Äúin online learning, where computer-mediated communication is heavily relied on, interruptions caused by communication overload are more likely to happen and can occur in various forms, such as emails or messages sent by instructors, updated announcements in the online learning system, or even the reminders of the assignment deadline‚Äù (Conrad et al., 2022). In another study conducted on student's responses to abrupt online learning, we found research that states, ‚Äúafter the transition, the resources with the greatest decrease were student-organized study groups, peer-led team learning groups, and review sessions‚Äù (Jeffery et al., 2020). The aim with our mobile application, Class+, was to help smooth their transition to this new environment by providing them with resources and ways to increase their motivation, engagement and overall participation; improving their work efficiency, accuracy, and performance. The reason behind our decision to target university students as our core users was because when the pandemic first began, they had to transition and adjust to this new learning environment in the middle of their academic year. Not to mention, students transitioning from high school course loads were also suddenly thrown into a university setting that was also learning to adapt to the new changes/environment. It can be argued that as new students weren't as familiar with the resources available to them, it made it even more difficult for them to access through remote means.
